My Story

How I Fell Into the Espresso Hole
(and haven't climbed out yet)

Let me be clear: six months ago, my entire coffee knowledge was "large iced vanilla latte from Starbucks, please." I thought espresso was just strong coffee. I thought tamping was something you did to a printer. I was, in every measurable way, wrong about all of it.

"I thought tamping was something you did to a printer."

It started, as most expensive hobbies do, with a YouTube rabbit hole at 11pm. One video about espresso extraction led to another about grind size, which led to a third about pressure profiling, and suddenly it was 2am and I had seventeen browser tabs open and was genuinely considering spending $3,000 on a machine I didn't know how to use.

I landed on the Breville Barista Express BES870XL — a machine that has a built-in grinder, which felt like cheating in the best way. I ordered it, waited four agonising days, set it up on my counter, and pulled my first shot.

"It tasted like someone filtered motor oil through regret."

It was bad. Like, genuinely bad. Sour and thin and wrong. I had done everything the manual said. I was baffled. Turns out there's a whole science to this — grind size, dose weight, tamp pressure, water temperature, extraction time — and I had accidentally gotten most of them wrong simultaneously. Great start.

But here's the thing: the second shot was slightly less bad. The fifth was okay. By shot thirty I was starting to understand what "dialing in" actually meant — it's that process of adjusting and tweaking until the shot is right. And I was hooked. Not just on the coffee, but on the problem of it. Every shot is a tiny experiment.

This site is my attempt to document everything I'm learning — the beans, the technique, the tools, the disasters — so that maybe you don't have to Google as frantically as I did. Fair warning: I am still very much figuring this out. But confused and honest beats polished and vague any day.

☕ Pull a Shot — Step by Step
01 Grind your beans ↓

Lock your portafilter into the grinder port, select your grind size (start around 8–10), and press the grind button. You're aiming for about 18–20g of grounds. The machine has a built-in timer — adjust until the dose is right.

💡 Dial In Tip: Grind size is your biggest lever. Finer = slower extraction (richer, more bitter). Coarser = faster (lighter, more sour).
02 Distribute the grounds ↓

After grinding, tap the portafilter lightly on the counter to settle the grounds. Use your finger or a distribution tool to level them. Uneven grounds = uneven extraction = bad coffee. This step matters way more than I initially thought.

💡 Dial In Tip: Some people use a WDT tool (Weiss Distribution Technique) — basically a needle stirrer — to break up clumps. You can DIY one with a cork and some needles.
03 Tamp the puck ↓

Place the portafilter on a stable surface. Hold the tamper like a door handle — don't grip it like a weapon. Press straight down with about 15–20kg of pressure (roughly: firm, but not furious). The goal is a flat, level puck with no cracks.

💡 Dial In Tip: Angle is everything. A tilted tamp = channeling, where water finds the easiest path through the puck instead of saturating it evenly. That's how you get sour, weak shots.
04 Lock in & pre-infuse ↓

Lock the portafilter into the group head and start the shot. The Barista Express has a pre-infusion mode — it gently saturates the puck at low pressure before ramping up. Watch the pressure gauge climb into the 8–9 bar zone.

💡 Dial In Tip: If pressure shoots above 12 bar — your grind is too fine. Below 6 bar — too coarse. Adjust one click at a time.
05 Time the extraction ↓

A good espresso pulls in 25–30 seconds from first drop to ~36–40ml in the cup. Under 20 seconds = underextracted (sour). Over 35 seconds = overextracted (bitter). Time everything. I use my phone. It feels ridiculous. It works.

💡 Dial In Tip: Watch the colour. It should flow dark brown, then honey, then lighten to pale yellow. Stop around 40ml or when it starts looking watery.
06 Steam your milk ↓

For lattes: use cold whole milk in a cold jug. Submerge the steam wand just below the surface, tip the jug slightly, and turn on steam. Lower the jug to introduce air (you'll hear a hissing tear sound). Once milk expands by ~30%, submerge deeper and spin for texture. Stop at 60–65°C.

💡 Dial In Tip: Microfoam should look like wet paint — glossy and smooth, not bubbly. If it looks frothy like a bubble bath, it's overfoamed and unusable for latte art.
